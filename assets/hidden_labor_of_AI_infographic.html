Data Labeling & AI Labor

The hidden labor of AI

Before an AI can speak, see, or reason — millions of humans must teach it. Their work is invisible. Their wages are not.

What is data labeling?
Raw data
Millions of text snippets, images, audio clips scraped from the internet
Source: the open web
Human labeler
Workers read, tag, categorize, and rank every piece — including graphic content
Often: Kenya, India, Philippines
Labeled dataset
Clean, categorized data used to train and "align" AI models like ChatGPT
Result: AI learns what's "safe"
AI product
Polished chatbot sold to millions — the human labor made invisible
Profit: Silicon Valley
The numbers
$1.32
Minimum take-home hourly wage for Kenyan data labelers working for OpenAI via Sama (2021–22)
$12.50
What OpenAI paid Sama per hour — 6–9× what workers actually received

Global data labeling market size in 2024 — projected to hit $134.7B by 2034

A worker's account
"That was torture. You will read a number of statements like that all through the week. By the time it gets to Friday, you are disturbed."
— Sama worker in Nairobi, Kenya, speaking to TIME Magazine (Jan. 2023). Workers were exposed to graphic descriptions of violence, self-harm, and child abuse — with limited access to mental health support.
Who holds the power?
AI companies (OpenAI, Google, Meta)
Set contracts without transparency
Do not disclose outsourcing partners
Capture nearly all profits
Distance themselves from labor conditions
Data labelers (Kenya, India, Philippines)
Contract workers, not employees
No job security or benefits
Exposed to traumatic content
Invisible in the AI "success story"

Hicks's "cis-tem" applied: AI systems are built with assumptions about whose labor is invisible. The infrastructure of "clean" AI depends on workers in the Global South absorbing harm so platforms stay profitable. Making this labor visible is itself a political act.
