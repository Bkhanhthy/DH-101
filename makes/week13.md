## Context Statement (~300 words)
The artifact is a federal job posting from March 2035, published by the "Human Continuity Institute," a fictional division of the US Office of Labor Realignment. The year is nine years after the widespread automation of creative and knowledge work. By this future, AI systems have so thoroughly displaced writers, designers, musicians, and journalists that the US government has passed the Federal Authenticity Standards Act (FASA-33) — a legal framework requiring that creative works, institutional decisions, and educational outputs be certified as "genuinely human" before carrying full legal and commercial standing.

The present trends that lead here are already visible today: the rapid displacement of creative workers by generative AI, growing anxiety about distinguishing human from machine output, and early legislative attempts (like the EU AI Act) to mandate disclosure of AI involvement. This future extrapolates those trends a decade forward — not into chaos, but into bureaucracy. Society has not collapsed; it has adapted, somewhat comfortably. There are government salaries, pension plans, wellness benefits, and a Director who sounds genuinely proud of the work.

The assumptions about human-AI relations embedded in this artifact are revealing. It assumes that "humanness" becomes a scarce, certifiable commodity — something that can be scored (the Verified Human Profile), audited, and legally attested. It assumes that humans will accept being measured for their humanity. It assumes that the solution to AI displacement is not to limit AI, but to formalize and monetize what AI cannot (yet) do. Most tellingly, the posting includes "dark time" — 10 days per year of unmonitored creative practice. The fact that unmonitored time is a listed benefit reveals the assumption that by 2035, almost all human creative activity is otherwise observed, logged, and evaluated.

## Critical Reflection (~250 words)
Is this future desirable? On the surface, yes — displaced workers have jobs, creativity is protected by law, and human experience is officially valued. The salary is good. The mission statement sounds noble. But the hidden costs accumulate quietly. When "being human" requires a government score, a biometric profile, and a federal certificate, humanity has been turned into a credential. The act of creating — historically private, messy, unverifiable — has become subject to audit. The most unsettling line in the posting is not a warning; it is a benefit: "dark time," unmonitored creative practice, offered as a perk. That this needs to be explicitly protected tells us everything.
This future reveals something urgent about the present: we are already building the infrastructure for it. AI detection tools exist now. Debates about AI disclosure in art, journalism, and academia are happening now. The impulse to certify human origin is already present in academic integrity policies, stock image licenses, and publishing contracts. The 2035 future is not a rupture — it is a continuation.

Is it avoidable? Partially. What would need to change is not primarily technological but political and cultural. We would need to resist the commodification of human experience — to refuse the logic that humanity is most valuable when it can be verified and sold. We would need labor protections that reduce displacement rather than just managing its aftermath. And we would need to ask, early, which things should never be subject to efficiency: grief, play, spontaneous creation, love. The job posting implicitly admits we forgot to ask in time.

## Reflection
I imagine AI futures as something in between — and that middle ground unsettles me more than either extreme. Pure utopia asks nothing of us; pure dystopia lets us off the hook by making collapse feel inevitable. The more honest future is bureaucratic and mundane: AI doesn't destroy humanity, it just quietly administers it. What that reveals about us today is uncomfortable — we are already optimizing, certifying, and commodifying human experience. We are already building the infrastructure for a world where "being human" needs a score. The future isn't coming. We are constructing it, incrementally, right now.
