## Design Statement
The Interview is a game about the performance of selfhood inside an automated system. You play a job applicant being evaluated entirely by an AI — no human face across the table, no small talk, no shared context. Just a screen, a timer, and a cursor that blinks whether you're ready or not.
The central question the game asks is simple: when a machine is watching, who do you become? Every choice in the game is a version of that question. Do you answer honestly or strategically? Do you push back against the system or move through it? Do you name the manipulation when you see it?
The choices matter because they reveal something about the player, not just the character. Choosing to perform — to say "driven, collaborative, results-oriented" — feels efficient and slightly gross. Choosing honesty feels vulnerable and slightly risky. That discomfort is the argument the game is making: in AI-mediated evaluation, authenticity itself becomes a strategy, and there's no clean way out of that.
The mechanic that supports this theme most directly is convergence. Both the honest path and the performance path lead to the same final question — because the system doesn't actually care which version of you showed up. It's collecting data either way. The rules of the game mirror the rules of the real system.
Ending A, where the player answers honestly and the AI responds with something like uncertainty, is the emotional core of the game. It doesn't promise a job. It doesn't promise anything. It just says: you said something real, and that happened. In a game about systems that reduce people to data points, that felt like the most honest ending I could write.

## Artist Statement 
Before this assignment, I thought of games as entertainment — something with winners and losers, points and progress bars. What I didn't think about was how games make arguments. The concept of procedural rhetoric changed how I see this: the rules of a game carry meaning, not just the story. A game where you can't win without compromising yourself is saying something about compromise. That's what I tried to build.
Working with AI as a collaborator was strange in a way that felt appropriate given the subject matter. I used Claude to draft the passage text and structure, then read through everything and made decisions about what to keep, what to change, and what felt true. The line "your identification of the system does not place you outside it" — that came from the AI, and I kept it because it said something I believed but hadn't articulated. That moment of recognizing your own idea in someone else's words is genuinely weird. I'm still thinking about it.
What I needed to do myself was decide what the game was for. The AI could generate text, but it couldn't tell me which ending mattered more, or why the honest answer felt more important than the clever one. That was a human judgment. I think that's the real lesson of this project: AI is very good at producing options, and humans are still responsible for choosing between them.
If I did this again, I'd add a third ending — one where you simply walk out mid-interview. I think that choice was missing.

## Attribution & AI Use
## How I Use AI

For this project, I used ChatGPT as a writing collaborator 
to help build my Twine interactive story, *The Interview*.

**What I asked AI to do:**
- Design the overall narrative structure and branching logic
- Draft all passage text based on my chosen concept (The AI Interview)
- Explain Twine syntax so I could implement it myself
- Help draft my Design and Artist Statements

**What I kept:**
- The core branching structure
- Most of the passage prose, which I read and revised carefully
- The line "your identification of the system does not place you 
outside it" — it felt true
- The AI's pause times (1.3s, 2.1s, 4.7s) — small but effective

**What I changed:**
- Wording in several passages to sound more like my voice
- Made the final call on which ending mattered most emotionally

**My policy on AI use:**
I think of AI as a very fast first draft. It gives me material to 
react to, which is easier than starting from nothing. But reacting 
is still a creative act — every keep/change/discard decision is mine.
