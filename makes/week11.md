# Week 11 – AI & Labor


## Research Notes (300 words)
Sources used:
The primary source for this project was the TIME Magazine investigation by Billy Perrigo (January 2023), "OpenAI Used Kenyan Workers on Less Than $2 Per Hour." This was the most detailed, firsthand account available — it included workers' payslips, internal Sama and OpenAI documents, and direct interviews with four Kenyan employees who spoke anonymously. It provided the core wage data ($1.32–$2.00/hr take-home vs. $12.50/hr billed to OpenAI) and documented the psychological conditions workers faced.
The second major source was Mary Gray and Siddharth Suri's book Ghost Work (2019), which gave theoretical grounding for the "ghost work" phenomenon — the structural invisibility of gig-based AI labor platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk, Appen, and Scale AI. Gray and Suri's framework helped explain why this labor disappears from public view by design, not by accident.

Market data came from Grand View Research and Market.us industry reports (2024), which confirmed the data labeling market was worth $19.7 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $134.7 billion by 2034 — contextualizing just how large and profitable this industry is while workers remain underpaid.

What was hardest to find:
Direct worker testimony was the most difficult material to locate. Most workers spoke anonymously out of fear of losing their jobs. Very little comes directly from workers themselves — most accounts are mediated through journalists or researchers.

What surprised me:

The most surprising finding was the deliberate supply chain structure. OpenAI didn't accidentally underpay workers — they routed labor through a contractor specifically marketed as "ethical," creating legal and reputational distance. The system was designed so that exploitation would be invisible. That intentionality was more disturbing than the wage number alone.

## Design Statement (250 words)

I chose the infographic format over a zine because my primary audience is classmates — people who use ChatGPT regularly but have likely never thought about who built it. An infographic communicates key facts quickly and in a format that is easy to share digitally, screenshot, or reference in conversation. A zine requires more sustained reading; an infographic can land its argument in under two minutes.
My audience is already inside the AI economy as users. That relationship is the entry point. The goal was not to make them feel guilty but to make the invisible visible — to give them a concrete, specific picture of the labor chain behind a product they interact with daily.

The color palette — black, white, and blue — was a deliberate choice. Blue is used exclusively for elements representing institutional power: section labels, the AI company column in the power analysis, the Hicks callout box, and the dominant bars in the wage chart. Gray and neutral tones represent workers and their conditions. This color logic encodes the power relationship without stating it explicitly — blue accumulates at the top of every section, mirroring how money and power accumulate at the top of the supply chain.

The wage comparison bar chart is the visual anchor of the entire infographic. I wanted one image that communicated the core argument at a glance. The $12.50 vs. $1.32 ratio does that more effectively than any paragraph could.
I want the audience to feel informed, not overwhelmed — and to leave with one number they cannot forget.

## Reflection
After researching this topic, I was genuinely surprised by how much human labor goes into something we think of as purely technological. The "invisible workers" of AI are mostly gig workers in countries like Kenya, India, and the Philippines who spend their days labeling data, filtering graphic content, and ranking AI responses — work that is essential but completely hidden from the public.
What struck me most was how deliberate that invisibility is. OpenAI didn't accidentally underpay workers — they routed labor through a contractor called Sama, which created distance between the company and the people actually doing the work. Workers took home as little as $1.32 an hour while OpenAI billed $12.50. That gap doesn't happen by mistake.
The profits go almost entirely to the companies at the top — OpenAI, investors, contractors — while the workers who made the product safe enough to sell to millions of people have no stake in any of it. No equity, no credit, no job security.
Hicks's idea of the "cis-tem" really clicked for me here. The system isn't broken — it's working exactly as designed, just for some people and not others. That was honestly the most uncomfortable takeaway from this project.
